STORYLINE



Indonesia has sent more troops to the troubled province of Aceh, bringing the numbers to over 30-thousand.



At the same time, international negotiators on Sunday made a last-ditch effort to save a peace pact in the restive province, despite the arrest of four rebel leaders accused of involvement in bombings across the archipelago.



The five-month-old agreement appeared to unravel in recent days, as Jakarta announced it was sending more troops to the province and readied a presidential decree allowing it to "launch a security operation" in the province.



Rebels issued a statement calling on their fighters to return to their bases and for citizens to halt all activities starting on Monday.



The rebels have between three and ten-thousand troops in the province.



The peace pact was signed on 9 December with much fanfare and in its first few months effectively ended the 26-year civil war on the province.



But violence intensified in the past two months, with both sides accusing the other of violating the agreement.



The government said rebels used the halt in fighting to promote independence, while the rebels accused the military of attacking supporters of the pact.



Police accused the rebels of being behind two bombings in Medan in north Sumatra, one outside a UN building in Jakarta and a fourth at the country's main airport.



Eleven people were injured in the blasts.



Police also said the four leaders of the Free Aceh Movement would be charged under a new anti-terrorism law.



They were detained on Friday as they attempted to leave the province.



The rebels condemned the arrests as "slanderous", but officials with the Geneva-based Henry Dunant Centre, which brokered the peace pact, remained hopeful the two sides could return to the negotiating table and avert another war.
